PAGE 3: CASE STUDY, BRAND ARCHITECTURE

URL: /work/brand-architecture
Attribution labels: SoundVision Technologies, Brand Architecture, 10 Years

Hero:
Title: "Two Brands. One Ecosystem."
Subhead: TruAudio and VSSL are complementary products sold through the same dealer channels by the same company. Keeping them distinct while making them work together required sustained, deliberate brand architecture thinking, maintained informally but consistently for over a decade.
Meta: Brands Managed: 2. Duration: 10 Years. Channels: Global. Parent: SVT.

Section 1, The Brand Structure:
Eyebrow: The Brand Structure
Headline: "One parent company. Two distinct brand identities."
Body: TruAudio and VSSL are not competing brands. They are complementary products: speakers need an amplifier, and an amplifier needs speakers. The brand architecture challenge was keeping them visually and tonally distinct while allowing them to coexist in the same dealer's portfolio and often the same installation.

Architecture diagram:
Parent bar (dark background): SoundVision Technologies
Two columns beneath:
Left (TruAudio, ink top border): Brand 01, TruAudio, "Built by installers, for installers." Rows: Category: Custom install speakers, CI and architectural audio. Audience: CI integrators, commercial installers, residential AV. Tone: Technical credibility, installer empathy, professional trust. Market: Heavily saturated, differentiated by founder origin story.
Right (VSSL, rust top border): Brand 02, VSSL, "Your music, everywhere in your home." Rows: Category: Distributed audio streaming, whole-home amplification. Audience: CI dealers, and end consumers via Amazon. Tone: Consumer simplicity, lifestyle aspiration, ease of use. Market: Emerging category, built from inception at SoundVision.
Shared strip beneath both columns: Same dealer network. Same distribution partners. Same sales team. Same install ecosystem. Products are complementary by design: TruAudio speakers require an amplifier, VSSL amplifiers require speakers.

Section 2, The Challenge:
Three cards:
01, Same channel, different message: Both brands go to market through the same CI dealer network. A dealer receives TruAudio content and VSSL content in the same week, sometimes the same email. The brand language had to be distinct enough that dealers instantly knew which brand they were looking at and what to do with it.
02, Two audiences, one brand: TruAudio's messaging speaks to the installer, addressing technical pain points that an end customer will never care about. VSSL's messaging speaks to the end customer's desire for simplicity. Writing for both without letting the tones bleed into each other required consistent discipline across every asset produced.
03, A new brand inside an established one: VSSL was built from inception inside SoundVision, a company that already had a strong TruAudio identity. Building a second brand from scratch without it feeling like a TruAudio sub-brand, while keeping the dealer relationships intact, required building a genuinely separate identity from day one.

Section 3, The Positioning:
Two column comparison:
TruAudio: Positioning statement: "Built by installers, for installers." Five bullets: Messaging leads with installer empathy, not product specs. Founders were CI integrators: they understand the job site, not just the product. Marketing addresses pain points the end customer will never notice but the installer lives with daily. In a saturated market, credibility is the differentiator. Tone: professional, technical, peer-to-peer.
VSSL: Positioning statement: "Your music, everywhere in your home." Five bullets: Messaging leads with the end-user experience, not the technical architecture. Consumer simplicity: phone to distributed audio system, no complexity visible. Dealer-facing content emphasizes ease of sell and ease of install. Amazon channel requires consumer-grade clarity and lifestyle framing. Tone: approachable, aspirational, lifestyle-forward.

Section 4, Channel Strategy:
Three cards: CI Dealer Network (both brands), Global Distribution (both brands), Amazon Consumer (VSSL only). Each with brand badges.

Section 5, Torus Relevance (dark background section):
Headline: "Torus has the same architecture challenge."
Body: Torus serves utilities, commercial and industrial customers, data centers, and government, four distinct audiences with different concerns, different trust signals, and different sales cycles, unified under one brand. Managing that without each audience feeling like they're getting someone else's message is exactly the problem I've been solving for a decade.
Three relevance cards: Multiple audiences one brand. Technical product non-technical message. Building a brand in a new category.

Note: Remove or generalize the Torus relevance section for other job applications. It is specific to this role.

Closing quote: "The hardest brand architecture problems aren't about aesthetics. They're about clarity under pressure, keeping every audience feeling like the brand was built specifically for them."

VIDEO DIRECTION SECTION (slot between Asset Inventory and Metrics):
Eyebrow: Video Direction
Headline: "Directed, produced, and appeared on camera."
Body: Video was not a separate workstream handed off to a production team. It was part of the same creative direction process as every other asset in the launch, briefed, overseen, and in many cases personally presented by the same person running the rest of the campaign.

Two column layout:
Left column, four video types listed:
01, Teaser and launch videos: Pre-launch teasers released across social channels to build anticipation before the MX Series was publicly announced. Creative direction from concept through final cut.
02, Product overview videos: Full product introductions for the MA.1 streaming amplifier and MS.1 dedicated WiFi streamer. On-camera presenting, demonstrating, and explaining each product's value to dealers and end customers.
03, App walkthrough and demo videos: Screen and on-camera walkthroughs of the VSSL app experience. Designed to show ease of use to both dealers selling the system and customers setting it up.
04, Tech support how-to series: Post-launch video series developed in direct collaboration with the tech support team. Goal: proactively answer the most common support calls before customers needed to make them, reducing inbound support volume.

Right column, three role cards:
Creative direction: Briefed, directed, and approved all video content working with an in-house part-time videographer. Every video reflects the same brand voice and visual standards as the rest of the launch materials.
In-house, not agency: All video produced internally. No agency involvement, no outside production budget. The full production process, from brief to final export, was managed internally with a part-time videographer and direct creative oversight.
10 videos and counting: Approximately 10 videos produced to date across all four types. The tech support series is ongoing, with new videos added as new support patterns emerge post-launch.

On-camera callout (dark background):
Headline: "Not just directing. Presenting."
Body: For the product overview and app walkthrough videos, I appear on camera as the presenter: demonstrating the product, walking through the app interface, and explaining features directly to the viewer. This was a deliberate choice. A familiar face builds trust with dealers and customers in a way that voiceover alone does not. It also keeps production lean and authentic.

Strategy note callout (rust left border):
Label: The strategic thinking behind the tech support series
Text: Most post-launch video programs are about awareness or sales. The tech support series was about a different outcome entirely: reducing the volume of inbound support calls by answering common questions before customers needed to ask them. Working directly with the support team to identify the highest-volume call topics, then building video content around those specific issues, treated video as a support channel, not just a marketing one.

PAGE 8: CASE STUDY, GO-TO-MARKET FRAMEWORK

URL: /work/gtm-framework
Attribution: SoundVision Technologies / VSSL, Marketing Strategy

Hero:
Title: "A Repeatable Launch Framework"
Subhead: Every product launch I run follows the same four-phase process: discovery, strategy, planning, and execution with measurement. This is the framework itself, followed by exactly how it played out for the VSSL MX Series, including the presale target I set before launch and the result that beat it.

Part 01, The Framework:
Four phase cards (light background):
01, Discovery: I always ask, what's the customer signal telling us, what gap exists in the category, who specifically needs this. Output: Opportunity brief.
02, Strategy: I always define, who are the distinct audience segments, how should we be positioned, what's the core message for each segment. Output: Positioning and segments.
03, Planning: I always sequence, which channel reaches which segment first, what's the logical order of disclosure, where does budget create the most leverage. Output: Channel and budget plan.
04, Execution + Measurement: I always set, what specific number defines success, what do we track during launch, what do we carry into post-launch. Output: Targets set before launch.

Part 02, Applied to MX Series:
Same four phases, dark background cards filled with real MX Series decisions:
Discovery: The signal, the MS.1 had been requested for years. The gap, no dedicated streaming solution existed in the line.
Strategy: Three segments defined, distributors, direct dealers, end customers. Positioning carried informally through creative briefs.
Planning: Press kits to 15 global distribution partners, followed by outreach to 1,000+ direct dealers.
Execution: Presale unit target set before launch. Result: exceeded, sold out via distribution alone.

Honest note callout (rust left border): The plan assumed distribution and direct dealers would receive inventory on a similar timeline. Demand through distribution exceeded that plan, selling out the first production batch before direct dealers could order at all. That wasn't the designed outcome, it was the framework correctly identifying real demand, and reality moving even faster than the plan accounted for. The launch date was adjusted by 30 days to rebuild inventory and protect the direct dealer experience.

Part 03, The Result:
Before/after panel: Pre-Launch Target, Set (specific presale unit goal defined before launch). Arrow. Actual Result, Exceeded (target surpassed, batch sold out via distribution).

Closing quote: "A plan is only worth something if it predicts reality. This one did, and reality still found a way to outrun it."

Link at bottom: "See the full execution: VSSL MX Series Launch Case Study" linking to /work/vssl-mx-series.


PAGE 9: CASE STUDY, PERSONAL BRAND SYSTEM

URL: /work/brand-system
Label: Self-initiated (not "client work," not "SoundVision")

Hero:
Eyebrow: Case Study, Personal Brand System
Title: "Building a brand from zero"
Subhead: A self-directed exercise: build a complete brand identity system, mark, color, typography, voice, with the same rigor I bring to company and employer brands.
Meta: Type: Brand Identity System. Scope: Full System. Role: Sole Designer and Strategist.
Hero visual: The DOI mark SVG large, centered, ink color fill on cream background.

Section 1, The Exercise:
Headline: "Why I built this"
Body: After years of building brand systems for other companies, I wanted to apply the same process to myself, start with nothing, and make every decision deliberately: a logo mark, a color palette, a typographic system, and a voice. The goal wasn't just a nice-looking result. It was to demonstrate the full process, end to end, the same way I'd approach it for an employer's brand.
Two cards: The Brief (to myself): Design a personal mark that works at any size, from a favicon to a large format print, and carries meaning beyond the surface. It needed to feel intentional, not decorative. The Approach: Start with the mark, build outward. Every subsequent decision, color, type, voice, had to support and reinforce the mark's geometry and meaning.

Section 2, The Mark (2x2 grid):
01, Surface read: A clean, modern geometric mark built to scale. Works at 16px as a favicon or at large format. Designed with swag in mind from day one.
02, Hidden initials: The mark spells my initials, D, O, I, encoded directly into the geometry. The white strokes and negative space form the letters without being literal.
03, Personal layer: Two hidden shapes in the geometry reference my two sons. A detail nobody notices unless they're told, but one that makes the mark mine.
04, The takeaway: A mark with this much intention demonstrates how I approach design generally. Surface-level polish backed by decisions that can be explained and defended.

Section 3, Color System:
Five swatches: #0A0B0C (Ink, primary background), #2F3237 (Charcoal, cards), #751D2F (Crimson, accent), #B0B3B8 (Silver, secondary text), #F7F7F8 (Off-White, primary text on dark).
Body: A restrained palette built around one accent color used sparingly, for emphasis, never for large fills. The goal was a system where every color choice felt deliberate, not decorative.

Section 4, Typography:
Three columns: Bebas Neue (Display), Cormorant Garamond italic (Editorial), Montserrat Light (Body and UI). Each with type specimen and usage description.

Closing quote: "A brand system built the way I'd build one for a team. Every decision intentional, every detail able to be explained."

Note: Do not include any pricing, services, or business language in this case study. It is a design exercise for an employer audience, not a client pitch.
